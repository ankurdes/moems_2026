How students should use this

Fast recognitionUse before hints

When do I use choose, factorial, or stars and bars?

A problem asks you to count possibilities. How do you know whether to use C(n,k), factorials, or stars and bars?
Show trick and tips
  • Use choose when order does not matter: choosing a team, committee, set of points, or subset.
  • Use factorial/permutations when order matters: arranging seats, lining people up, ordering digits, or assigning 1st/2nd/3rd roles.
  • Use stars and bars when identical things are being distributed into labeled boxes: candies to kids, points split among teams, digits/variables adding to a total.
  • Use casework when there are restrictions that break a clean formula: digit caps, “at least,” “not adjacent,” or “distinct.”
Show examples
  • Choose 3 people from 7: C(7,3), because a team has no order.
  • Arrange 5 people in a line: 5!, because order matters.
  • Give 8 identical candies to 3 kids: solve x+y+z=8, so stars and bars gives C(10,2).
  • Three digits add to 12: stars and bars first, then subtract digit values above 9.

Stars and bars quick test

How many nonnegative integer solutions are there to a+b+c+d=15?
Show first hint
imagine 15 stars and 3 dividers creating 4 groups.
Show trick and tips
  • For k variables adding to n, nonnegative solutions are C(n+k-1,k-1).
  • If every variable must be positive, give each variable 1 first, then distribute the leftover.
  • If variables have upper limits, count unrestricted first and subtract illegal cases.
Show solution path

There are 15 stars and 3 bars, so choose the 3 bar positions among 18 total positions: C(18,3)=816.

Answer: 816.

Fast factor counting from prime powers

How many positive divisors does 7560 have?
Show first hint
prime-factor the number, then add 1 to each exponent and multiply.
Show trick and tips
  • If N = p^a q^b r^c, then each divisor chooses exponent 0 through a for p, exponent 0 through b for q, etc.
  • That gives (a+1)(b+1)(c+1) total divisors.
  • To list divisors quickly, build from the prime-power choices instead of testing every number.
Show solution path
  1. 7560 = 756 × 10 = (2² × 3³ × 7) × (2 × 5).
  2. So 7560 = 2³ × 3³ × 5¹ × 7¹.
  3. Number of divisors is (3+1)(3+1)(1+1)(1+1)=4×4×2×2=64.

Answer: 64.

Useful quick triggers

When reading a problem, what words should make you think, “There is probably a shortcut here”?
Show shortcut map

1. “Divisors” or “factors”

What it means: The problem is asking about numbers that divide evenly into another number.

Why prime factorization helps: Every divisor is built from the prime ingredients of the number. Once you know the prime ingredients, you can count choices instead of guessing factors.

How to do it: Prime-factor the number. For each prime, count how many exponent choices you have: from 0 up to the exponent.

Example: 360 = 2³ × 3² × 5¹. A divisor can use:

  • 2⁰, 2¹, 2², 2³ → 4 choices
  • 3⁰, 3¹, 3² → 3 choices
  • 5⁰, 5¹ → 2 choices

Total divisors: 4 × 3 × 2 = 24.

Watch out: The exponent 0 means “do not use that prime.” That is why we add 1 to each exponent.

2. “Perfect square” or “perfect cube”

What it means: The number must be something like m² or m³.

Why exponents matter: When you square or cube a number, all prime exponents get multiplied by 2 or 3.

Square rule: A perfect square has only even exponents. Example: 2⁴ × 3² × 7⁶.

Cube rule: A perfect cube has exponents that are multiples of 3. Example: 2³ × 5⁶ × 11⁹.

Example: Make 72N a perfect square. Since 72 = 2³ × 3², the 2³ is odd and needs one more 2. The 3² is already even. So the least N is 2.

Watch out: Do not multiply by the whole number again. Only multiply by the missing prime pieces.

3. “At least one”

What it means: The problem allows 1, 2, 3, or more successful things. Counting all those cases separately may be annoying.

Shortcut idea: Count the opposite: “none.” Then subtract from all possibilities.

Why it works: Everything either has at least one success or has zero successes. There is no overlap.

Example: In 5 coin flips, how many outcomes have at least one head?

  • All outcomes: 2⁵ = 32
  • Opposite: no heads, only TTTTT → 1 outcome
  • At least one head: 32 − 1 = 31

Watch out: Use this when the opposite is simpler than the original.

4. “A or B” in a counting problem

What it means: You are combining two groups. Some items might belong to both groups.

Shortcut idea: Count group A, count group B, then subtract the overlap once.

Why it works: Items in both groups get counted twice when you add A + B.

Example: How many numbers from 1 to 100 are divisible by 5 or 7?

  • Divisible by 5: 20
  • Divisible by 7: 14
  • Divisible by both 5 and 7 means divisible by 35: 2
  • Total: 20 + 14 − 2 = 32

Watch out: In math counting, “or” usually includes things that satisfy both unless the problem says “but not both.”

5. “Consecutive integers”

What it means: The numbers are in a row with no gaps, like 18,19,20,21,22.

Shortcut idea: Use sum = average × number of terms.

Why it works: Consecutive integers balance around the middle. The first and last average to the same middle as every other pair.

Example: 18+19+20+21+22 has middle/average 20 and 5 terms, so the sum is 20×5=100.

Even-number example: 7+8+9+10 has average (7+10)/2 = 8.5 and 4 terms, so the sum is 8.5×4=34.

Watch out: For an even number of terms, the average may be a half number. That is okay.

6. “Remainder”

What it means: The problem cares about what is left after division.

Shortcut idea: Numbers with the same remainder form a pattern.

Why it works: If a number leaves remainder 2 when divided by 9, then adding 9 keeps the same remainder.

Example: Numbers that leave remainder 2 when divided by 9 are 2, 11, 20, 29, 38, .... You jump by 9 each time.

How to use it: Instead of checking every number, write the pattern 9k+2 and use the size limit from the problem.

Watch out: Remainder must be smaller than the divisor. Remainder 9 when dividing by 9 is really remainder 0.

7. “Around a circle”

What it means: Rotating the whole arrangement does not make a new arrangement.

Shortcut idea: Fix one object in place, then arrange the rest.

Why it works: In a line, there is a first spot. In a circle, there is no true first spot, so rotations would be overcounted.

Example: 5 people around a table is not 5!. Fix one person’s chair. Arrange the other 4 people: 4! = 24.

Watch out: If the problem says bracelets or necklaces, flipping may also count as the same. Then there is usually another division by 2.

8. “Digits”

What it means: The value of each digit depends on its position.

Shortcut idea: Translate the digits into place value or digit conditions.

Place value: A three-digit number ABC means 100A + 10B + C, not A×B×C.

Digit sum: If the problem asks for the sum of digits, use A+B+C.

Divisibility tips: For divisibility by 4, only the last two digits matter. For divisibility by 9, the digit sum matters. For divisibility by 5, the last digit is 0 or 5.

Example: If ABC is divisible by 4, only BC must be divisible by 4. You do not need to test the whole number.

Watch out: The first digit of a multi-digit number cannot be 0, unless the problem is using leading zeros as placeholders.

1. Two-digit divisors

How many two-digit positive divisors does 2024 have?
Show first hint
do not start by testing 10, 11, 12, 13... Find the structure of 2024.
Show trick and tips
  • When a problem asks for divisors, prime factor first.
  • Every divisor must be built from the prime pieces of the number.
  • After factoring, list only divisors in the range 10 to 99.
Show solution path
  1. 2024 = 8 × 253 = 2³ × 11 × 23.
  2. Every divisor has form 2^a × 11^b × 23^c, where a=0,1,2,3 and b,c=0,1.
  3. The two-digit divisors are 11, 22, 23, 44, 46, 88, 92.

Answer: 7.

2. Sum of divisors

Find the sum of all positive divisors of 2023.
Show first hint
factor 2023. Then avoid listing every divisor one by one.
Show trick and tips
  • If n = p^a q^b, every divisor is made by choosing a power of p and a power of q.
  • The sum of all divisors is (1+p+...+p^a)(1+q+...+q^b).
  • This works because multiplying the parentheses creates every possible divisor exactly once.
Show solution path
  1. 2023 = 7 × 17².
  2. Sum of divisors is (1+7)(1+17+17²).
  3. 1+17+289 = 307, so the sum is 8 × 307 = 2456.

Answer: 2456.

3. Perfect cube completion

What is the least positive integer N such that 96N is a perfect cube?
Show first hint
for a perfect cube, what must happen to every prime exponent?
Show trick and tips
  • For a perfect square, exponents must be even.
  • For a perfect cube, exponents must be multiples of 3.
  • Multiply only by the missing prime powers, not by the whole number again.
Show solution path
  1. 96 = 2⁵ × 3¹.
  2. 2⁵ needs one more factor of 2 to become 2⁶.
  3. 3¹ needs two more factors of 3 to become 3³.
  4. So N = 2 × 3² = 18.

Answer: 18.

4. Coin equation / remainder shortcut

Nonnegative integers X,Y satisfy 20X + 23Y = 2023. Find the least possible value of X + Y.
Show first hint
if you want fewer total terms, should you use more 20s or more 23s?
Show trick and tips
  • Since 23 is larger than 20, using more Ys usually reduces X+Y.
  • But 2023 - 23Y must be divisible by 20.
  • Use remainders to find which Y values are allowed.
Show solution path
  1. 20X = 2023 - 23Y, so 2023 - 23Y must be divisible by 20.
  2. Modulo 20: 2023 ≡ 3 and 23Y ≡ 3Y.
  3. So 3 - 3Y ≡ 0 mod 20, meaning Y ≡ 1 mod 20.
  4. The largest possible Y is 81.
  5. X = (2023 - 23×81)/20 = 8, so X+Y = 89.

Answer: 89.

5. Telescoping product

Evaluate (1+1/1)(1+1/2)(1+1/3)...(1+1/2021).
Show first hint
rewrite each factor as one fraction before multiplying.
Show trick and tips
  • Products with neighboring numbers often telescope.
  • Write 1 + 1/k as (k+1)/k.
  • Then look for top/bottom cancellation.
Show solution path
  1. The product becomes 2/1 × 3/2 × 4/3 × ... × 2022/2021.
  2. Every middle number cancels.
  3. Only 2022/1 remains.

Answer: 2022.

6. Identity instead of expansion

Simplify [(2024−2025)² − 2024² − 2025²] / (2024×2025).
Show first hint
replace 2024 and 2025 with a and b. What identity appears?
Show trick and tips
  • Do not square 2024 and 2025.
  • Use (a-b)² = a² - 2ab + b².
  • Big numbers are often there to tempt you into unnecessary arithmetic.
Show solution path
  1. Let a=2024 and b=2025.
  2. (a-b)² - a² - b² = a² - 2ab + b² - a² - b² = -2ab.
  3. Divide by ab to get -2.

Answer: -2.

7. Alternating sum

Find 1 − 2 + 3 − 4 + ... + 2023.
Show first hint
group terms in pairs. What does each pair equal?
Show trick and tips
  • Long alternating sums usually pair nicely.
  • 1−2=-1, 3−4=-1, and so on.
  • Watch for the leftover final term because 2023 is odd.
Show solution path
  1. (1−2) + (3−4) + ... + (2021−2022) + 2023.
  2. There are 1011 pairs of -1.
  3. Total is 2023 - 1011 = 1012.

Answer: 1012.

8. Percent product

If C = A × B², and A increases by 10% while B increases by 20%, by what percent does C increase?
Show first hint
use multiplication factors, not “10 + 20”.
Show trick and tips
  • A 10% increase means multiply by 1.10.
  • A 20% increase in B means B² is multiplied by 1.20².
  • When variables multiply, percentage changes multiply too.
Show solution path
  1. New A is 1.10A.
  2. New B² is (1.20B)² = 1.44B².
  3. New C is multiplied by 1.10 × 1.44 = 1.584.
  4. The increase is 0.584 = 58.4%.

Answer: 58.4%.

9. Digit sum with a cap

How many positive integers less than 1000 have digits that add to 12?
Show first hint
pretend leading zeros are allowed, then correct for digits that are too large.
Show trick and tips
  • Use digit slots a,b,c with a+b+c=12.
  • First count nonnegative solutions without the digit limit.
  • Then subtract cases where a digit is 10 or more.
Show solution path
  1. Allow leading zeros, so every number below 1000 is represented by three digits.
  2. Without digit caps, a+b+c=12 has C(14,2)=91 solutions.
  3. If one digit is at least 10, subtract 10 from it. Then the remaining sum is 2, giving C(4,2)=6 cases for that digit.
  4. There are 3 choices for which digit is too large, so subtract 3×6=18.
  5. 91-18=73.

Answer: 73.

10. Grid rectangles

How many rectangles of size 2×3 can be placed inside an 8×8 grid if the rectangle must follow the grid lines?
Show first hint
count one orientation, then remember the rotated orientation.
Show trick and tips
  • A w×h rectangle fits in an 8×8 grid in (8-w+1)(8-h+1) positions.
  • Because 2×3 and 3×2 are different orientations, count both.
Show solution path
  1. For 2-wide and 3-tall: (8-2+1)(8-3+1)=7×6=42.
  2. For 3-wide and 2-tall: 6×7=42.
  3. Total is 42+42=84.

Answer: 84.

11. Products of three primes

How many products of three distinct prime numbers are less than or equal to 100?
Show first hint
start with the smallest possible primes. Once a product is too large, larger primes will also be too large.
Show trick and tips
  • This is controlled casework, not a formula problem.
  • Use smallest primes first: 2,3,5,7,11,13,17....
  • Stop a branch as soon as the product exceeds 100.
Show solution path
  1. With 2 and 3, the third prime can be 5,7,11,13. 2×3×17=102 is too large. That gives 4.
  2. Next try 2,5,7, which gives 70. But 2×5×11=110 is too large. That gives 1 more.
  3. Without 2, the smallest product is 3×5×7=105, already too large.

Answer: 5.

12. Circular arrangements

Five different points are placed around a circle. How many different orders of the points are possible around the circle?
Show first hint
rotations should not create new arrangements. Fix one point.
Show trick and tips
  • For circular arrangements, fix one object to remove rotations.
  • Then arrange the remaining objects normally.
  • For 5 objects around a circle, the count is (5-1)!.
Show solution path
  1. Fix one point at the top of the circle.
  2. Arrange the remaining 4 points: 4! = 24.

Answer: 24.

13. Square-root side lengths

A triangle has side lengths √8, √18, and √26. Find its area.
Show first hint
square the side lengths. Do they reveal a right triangle?
Show trick and tips
  • When side lengths are square roots, compare the squared lengths.
  • If the two smaller squared lengths add to the largest squared length, use the Pythagorean theorem.
  • Then area is 1/2 × leg × leg.
Show solution path
  1. The squared lengths are 8, 18, 26.
  2. Since 8+18=26, it is a right triangle.
  3. Area is 1/2 × √8 × √18 = 1/2 × √144 = 6.

Answer: 6.

14. Ant on a cube

An ant crawls from one corner of a unit cube to the opposite corner on the surface. What is the shortest possible path length?
Show first hint
the ant is walking on surfaces, so unfold the cube into a flat picture.
Show trick and tips
  • Surface shortest-path problems usually become straight-line problems after unfolding.
  • Unfold two adjacent unit faces into a 2×1 rectangle.
  • The shortest path is the rectangle’s diagonal.
Show solution path
  1. Unfold two adjacent faces into a rectangle of dimensions 2 and 1.
  2. The straight-line diagonal has length √(2²+1²).
  3. That is √5.

Answer: √5.

15. Consecutive sums

Find the smallest positive integer that can be written as a sum of 3 consecutive whole numbers, 4 consecutive whole numbers, and 5 consecutive whole numbers.
Show first hint
write formulas for sums of 3, 4, and 5 consecutive numbers.
Show trick and tips
  • A sum of 3 consecutive whole numbers is divisible by 3.
  • A sum of 5 consecutive whole numbers is divisible by 5.
  • A sum of 4 consecutive whole numbers has form 4k+6, so test multiples of 15 until the 4-term condition works.
Show solution path
  1. The number must be divisible by both 3 and 5, so test multiples of 15.
  2. 15 cannot be 4k+6 because 4k=9.
  3. 30 works: 9+10+11=30, 6+7+8+9=30, and 4+5+6+7+8=30.

Answer: 30.

16. Prime pair trap

Prime numbers p<q satisfy p+q=2383. Find q.
Show first hint
2383 is odd. What does that force about one of the primes?
Show trick and tips
  • All primes except 2 are odd.
  • Odd + odd = even, so two odd primes cannot sum to an odd number.
  • Therefore one prime must be 2.
Show solution path
  1. Since 2383 is odd, one prime must be 2.
  2. Because p<q, p=2.
  3. q=2383-2=2381.

Answer: 2381.

17. Consecutive integers with a negative sum

What is the greatest possible number of consecutive integers whose sum is -24?
Show first hint
a very long list can have a small sum if positive and negative terms cancel.
Show trick and tips
  • Pair negative and positive opposites: -k and k cancel.
  • To get a negative leftover, start one step farther on the negative side.
  • The list -24 through 23 is a strong candidate.
Show solution path
  1. Use the consecutive integers -24,-23,...,-1,0,1,...,22,23.
  2. Every pair -k and k cancels, leaving only -24.
  3. From -24 to 23 inclusive, there are 48 integers.

Answer: 48.

18. Divisibility by 5 in a product

How many positive integers n make (n+1)(n+2)(n+3) divisible by 5, for 1 ≤ n ≤ 20?
Show first hint
a product is divisible by 5 if at least one factor is a multiple of 5.
Show trick and tips
  • Check residues of n modulo 5.
  • You need n+1, n+2, or n+3 to be divisible by 5.
  • There are four complete groups of five numbers from 1 to 20.
Show solution path
  1. n+1,n+2,n+3 is divisible by 5 when n ≡ 4,3,2 mod 5.
  2. That gives 3 good residues out of every 5.
  3. From 1 to 20 there are 4 full cycles, so the count is 4×3=12.

Answer: 12.
